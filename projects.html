Projects & Case Studies

These are the projects that best represent how I think, build, and lead — from creating a socially impactful emergency app concept to breaking down a real firearm supply chain and exploring psychology in UX design. Each case study follows a Situation, Task, Action, and Results structure so you can see not just what I did, but how I approached it.

Dial – Silent Emergency Response App

Entrepreneurship Class Individual Pitch · Junior Year
Entrepreneurship Product Strategy Business Model Canvas Public Speaking

Situation

In my entrepreneurship class, we were challenged to create a realistic business idea and pitch it to the entire class. I didn’t want to build just another convenience app — I wanted something with real impact. After looking into gaps in public safety technology, I realized how many people, like domestic-violence victims, students, and gig workers, don’t always have the ability to safely call 911. That insight sparked the idea for Dial: a silent emergency-alert app.

Task

My task was to turn that rough idea into a complete, credible business pitch. That meant defining the problem, mapping target users, sizing the market, designing core features, and building a revenue model that served both everyday users and government agencies — all in a way that felt grounded, not gimmicky.

Actions

I researched how emergency response systems work today and built Dial’s value proposition around what they miss: silent activation, automatic GPS location, and instant camera/mic capture for evidence. I mapped out B2C customer segments (victims, students, gig workers, people with disabilities) and B2G customers (police, EMS, public safety departments). I used a business model canvas to connect key activities, partners, and resources, and I built a slide deck that summarized everything from TAM/SAM/TM to pricing strategy: a low-friction subscription for individuals and licensing contracts for agencies.

Results

The pitch landed really well. My classmates and professor said it felt both innovative and practical and highlighted the social impact as a differentiator. The project gave me confidence in turning a vague idea into a structured business concept and showed I could communicate a complex, sensitive topic in a way that felt clear, human, and actionable.

Key skills: problem framing, market sizing, business model design, deck building, public speaking.

Remington 870 – Supply Chain Case Study

Supply Chain Management Course · Sophomore Year
Supply Chain Supplier Strategy KPI Analysis Risk Management

Situation

In my Supply Chain Management course, our final case study required us to pick a real product with a complex production and logistics process and break down its entire supply chain. I chose the Remington 870 shotgun because it has a long history, multiple specialized components, and a mix of domestic and global suppliers — enough moving parts to really test my ability to map an end-to-end supply chain.

Task

My task was to build a full report covering demand, supplier strategy, quality management, inventory planning, global sourcing, KPIs, risk mitigation, and future recommendations. The goal wasn’t just to describe the current state, but to evaluate it and propose realistic, data-backed improvements that Remington could act on.

Actions

I started by mapping each major component — steel for receivers and barrels, wooden and synthetic stocks, triggers, springs, and coatings — and identifying key supplier types, from Carpenter Technology and Criterion Barrels to stock manufacturers and coating providers. Using Porter’s Five Forces and voice-of-customer inputs, I identified where Remington needed to excel: reliability, on-time delivery, cost control, and regulatory compliance. From there, I built a supplier performance scorecard with KPIs like on-time delivery, defect rate, and cost variance, and called out a major issue: Criterion Barrels’ 5% defect rate versus a <1% target. I proposed supplier development programs, joint quality initiatives, and performance-based incentives to close that gap. I also analyzed inventory patterns across regions and seasons, explored global sourcing options for components, and mapped risks like material cost swings and shipping delays to mitigation strategies.

Results

The final report was thorough and well received. My professor specifically highlighted the supplier scorecard, the analysis of Criterion’s defect rate, and the global sourcing recommendations. The project helped me build confidence in breaking down a complicated supply chain into understandable pieces and reinforced how critical supplier relationships, quality control, and risk planning are in real operations work.

Key skills: supply chain mapping, KPI design, supplier scorecards, global sourcing, risk identification and mitigation.

Reverse Psychology in UX – Bias in Design Project

UX Design Course Visual Project · Senior Year
UX Design Psychology Visual Storytelling Canva

Situation

In my UX class, we were asked to pick a cognitive bias, UX law, or dark pattern and turn it into a visual educational piece — something like a LinkedIn carousel, Instagram reel, or TikTok short. The goal was to show how psychology influences design while applying CRAP principles for visual clarity. I chose Reverse Psychology in Design because I kept seeing it in everyday apps and wanted to unpack how it nudges user behavior.

Task

My task was to research reverse psychology, find credible sources and real-world examples, and transform that into a concise visual story. I needed to define the concept, explain why designers use it, show real examples, and end with ethical guidelines — all in a format that was easy to scan, visually consistent, and engaging.

Actions

I pulled from psychology and UX sources to define reverse psychology and why it drives engagement, then chose two concrete examples: Duolingo’s streak-loss notifications and AdBlock’s playful “Don’t install AdBlock” messaging. I storyboarded the flow — hook, definition, why it’s used, examples, and a “Do/Don’t” ethics slide — and then built the full carousel in Canva using a consistent blue palette, clear hierarchy, and strong contrast. Each screen focused on one idea, with short copy and supporting visuals. After a draft round of peer feedback, I revised the copy and layout to make the examples and takeaways even clearer.

Results

The final piece clearly showed how reverse psychology shows up in real interfaces and how it can quickly cross the line into manipulation if misused. I walked away with a stronger sense of how subtle design decisions influence user behavior and how to communicate complex concepts visually without overwhelming people. It also made me more aware of the psychological tricks I encounter in my own apps, which will make me a more thoughtful designer and consumer.

Key skills: UX research, visual communication, CRAP principles, ethical design thinking, Canva production.
