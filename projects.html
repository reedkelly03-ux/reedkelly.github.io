Dial: Silent Emergency-Alert App

Entrepreneurship · Junior Year Individual Pitch

Situation

In my entrepreneurship class, we had to create a realistic business idea and pitch it to the entire class. I didn’t want to build just another generic app. After looking into gaps in public safety technology, I realized how many people — like domestic-violence victims, students, and gig workers — don’t always have the ability to call 911 safely. That insight sparked the idea for Dial, a silent emergency-alert app.

Task

My job was to turn that rough idea into a complete, credible business pitch. That meant defining the market, designing the core features, and proving the concept could actually work in practice.

Actions

I researched how emergency response systems work today and used that to build out Dial’s value proposition. I mapped customer segments, tech requirements, and a revenue strategy that included individual subscriptions and licensing for government agencies. I also built a slide deck and practiced explaining the problem in a way that felt real and human, rather than overly technical.

Results

The pitch landed well. My classmates and professor said it felt both innovative and practical, and that the social impact made it stand out from other projects. The experience gave me more confidence in turning an idea into a structured business model and communicating a complex concept in a clear, purposeful way.

Remington 870 Supply Chain Case Study

Supply Chain Management · Sophomore Year Capstone Case

Situation

In my Supply Chain Management course, our final case study required us to select a real product with a complex production and logistics process and break down its entire supply chain. I chose the Remington 870 shotgun because of its long history, specialized components, and mix of domestic and global suppliers — enough moving parts to really test my analytical skills.

Task

I was tasked with creating an end-to-end supply chain report that covered demand, supplier strategy, quality management, inventory patterns, global sourcing, KPIs, risk mitigation, and potential improvement projects.

Actions

I mapped major components like steel, barrels, stocks, and triggers and identified where they came from and which suppliers were involved. I used Porter’s Five Forces to analyze the competitive landscape and the voice of the customer to highlight performance priorities. From there, I built a supplier scorecard, evaluated inventory by region and season, and explored global sourcing options for cost and reliability.

A key insight was that Criterion Barrels had a 5% defect rate — far above the <1% benchmark. I proposed supplier-development programs, joint quality initiatives, and performance-based incentives to improve consistency and reduce defect-related costs. I also identified risks like material cost fluctuations and shipping delays and paired each with practical mitigation strategies.

Results

The final report was well-received, and my professor highlighted the supplier quality analysis and global sourcing recommendations as strengths. The project reinforced how important supplier relationships, quality control, and risk planning are in real-world operations.

Reverse Psychology in UX Design

UXD Class · Bias in Design Visual Project

Situation

In my UX class, we were asked to choose a cognitive bias, UX law, or dark pattern and turn it into a visual educational piece (like a LinkedIn carousel or Instagram reel). I chose Reverse Psychology in Design because I kept seeing it in apps I use every day and wanted to understand how it nudges user behavior.

Task

My task was to research reverse psychology, find credible sources and real examples, and turn that into a clear, visually structured carousel. I needed to define the concept, explain why designers use it, show real examples, and end with guidance on using it ethically.

Actions

I researched the bias using psychology and UX sources, then collected examples like Duolingo’s streak-loss notifications and AdBlock’s “Don’t install AdBlock” prompt. I storyboarded the flow: a hook, definition, “why it’s used,” examples, and a do/don’t slide. Then I built the carousel in Canva, applying consistent colors, typography, and alignment while using contrast to highlight key messages.

Results

The final design clearly showed how reverse psychology appears in everyday interfaces and how easily it can cross the line into dark patterns if misused. I came away with a stronger understanding of the psychology behind interfaces and improved my visual storytelling and layout skills.
